Scoring Methodology

How SchoolChoice computes university admission probability — and why the numbers can be trusted

The Core Principle

Every admission probability in the system is computed from two things:

  1. The exact scoring formula published by each university for each programme
  2. The actual admission statistics (median and lower quartile scores) of students who were admitted in the most recent cycle

There is no machine learning, no training data, and no black-box model. The system applies each programme's published rules to the student's grades, computes a weighted score, and compares it against the real distribution of admitted students. The result is a statistical position — "where does this student sit relative to people who actually got in?"

Traceability: Every number in the system links to a specific official source — a JUPAS PDF, a university score calculator page, or a programme requirements document. The methodology report in the app cites these sources directly.

How Scoring Works

1

Convert grades to points

The student's HKDSE grades are converted to numerical points using the standard enhanced 8.5-point scale adopted by all JUPAS institutions from 2025. For example, a grade of 5** converts to 8.5 points, a grade of 4 converts to 4.0 points.

2

Apply programme-specific weights

Each programme specifies which subjects matter most. HKU Engineering weights Mathematics at 1.5x. CityU Biomedical Engineering weights Physics, Chemistry, and Biology at 2x. CUHK Computer Science weights M1/M2 at 1.75x. The system applies the exact published multiplier for each subject.

3

Select best subjects

Most programmes use a "best N" formula — select the N subjects with the highest weighted scores. Some add a fractional bonus for the next-best subject (e.g., HKU BBA adds 0.2x the 6th best subject). The system handles all published formula patterns.

4

Compare against admitted students

The student's total weighted score is compared against the published admission statistics for that programme — specifically the median and lower quartile of actually admitted students. This comparison produces an admission probability using piecewise interpolation between the known quantile points.

5

Flag risk level

Students scoring below the lower quartile are flagged as "at risk" — their grades put them below most students who were admitted to that programme. Students above the median are in a strong competitive position.

How Probability Is Computed

The raw weighted score needs to be converted into a meaningful probability. We use piecewise interpolation between known quantile points — the published median and lower quartile of admitted students.

Student Score PositionProbabilityWhat It Means
Above upper quartile75%+Stronger than most admitted students
At median~50%In line with the typical admitted student
At lower quartile~25%At the bottom of the admitted range
Below lower quartile<25%Below most admitted students — at risk

Between known quantile points, the system interpolates linearly. Above and below the known range, it uses exponential decay to approach (but never reach) 99% or 1%. The probability is always clamped to [1%, 99%] — the system never claims certainty in either direction.

What the probability means: "Based on grades alone, this is where the student sits in the distribution of previously admitted students." It is not a guarantee. Non-academic factors (interviews, portfolios, personal statements, band preference) also influence admission but are not modelled quantitatively because they have no published statistical weight.

Where AI Is Used (and Where It Isn't)

AI is NOT used for

Grade-to-score conversion. Subject weighting. Probability computation. Eligibility checking. Risk classification. Any quantitative recommendation.

These are all deterministic calculations using published formulas and verified data.

AI IS used for

Generating advisory language in academic plans — explaining why a programme is a good fit, suggesting preparation strategies, writing action items. The AI sees the quantitative results and writes human-readable commentary around them.

AI-generated text can be edited by the counsellor before sharing with students.

Annual Maintenance

The system requires one update per year, after JUPAS publishes new admission statistics (typically August):

  1. Download the new JUPAS Admissions Scores PDF
  2. Extract median and lower quartile per programme (automated script)
  3. Update programme data files
  4. Run validation tests (46 unit tests for the scoring engine)
  5. Check university websites for any formula changes (rare — typically stable year-to-year)

The entire update process takes approximately 2 hours. Scoring formulas change infrequently; admission statistics are the primary annual update.
